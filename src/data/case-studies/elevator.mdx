<TLDR>
  As part of a job interview, I explored how to improve the everyday elevator
  experience in residential buildings. Through user interviews, domain research,
  and insights from the psychology of waiting, I identified uncertainty during
  waiting as the core problem. I then designed a set of environmental,
  informational, and accessible UI interventions aimed at reducing perceived
  waiting time.
</TLDR>

## Context

This was a conceptual design exercise completed during a job interview. Rather than focusing on technical optimisation or new hardware, I treated the elevator as a product experience and explored how research and psychology could inform early strategic design decisions.

## Research

I started by learning about elevators and people’s experiences with them.

<HighlightList>
  <Highlight title="User interviews" icon="questionAnswer">
    I interviewed 9 people, who described a wide range of frustrations. However,
    waiting for the elevator was by far their biggest complaint.
  </Highlight>
  <Highlight title="Domain research" icon="upDown">
    To understand which aspects of the experience could realistically be
    influenced, I read 20 articles on elevator design and operation. The main
    insight was that user satisfaction is more strongly influenced by perceived
    waiting time than actual speed.
  </Highlight>
  <Highlight title="Psychology of waiting" icon="hourglass">
    I read up on the psychology of waiting. I learned that many factors
    influence our perception of waiting time. For instance, waiting time seems
    longer when we don’t know how long it’ll be, what is causing it, or when
    it’s perceived as unfair.
  </Highlight>
</HighlightList>

## Framing the challenge

To ground the concept, I first defined the following assumptions and constraints:

<HighlightList>
  <Highlight title="Client" icon="mitsubishi">
    Mitsubishi, seeking differentiation through experience rather than speed
  </Highlight>
  <Highlight title="Setting" icon="lock">
    Luxury residential buildings with 4–12 floors, anywhere in the world
  </Highlight>
  <Highlight title="Users" icon="groups">
    Residents of all ages, including people with sensory, motor, or other
    impairments
  </Highlight>
  <Highlight title="Technical constriants" icon="groups">
    A _collective control_ algorithm, meaning the elevator only changes
    direction when all requests in the current direction have been served
  </Highlight>
</HighlightList>

I then distilled these requirements and my research into a problem statement:

> How might we improve the elevator experience of residents of 4‑to‑12‑story luxury apartment buildings by reducing perceived waiting time?

## Translating insights into design decisions

I translated my research into a set of environmental, informational, and accessible interventions aimed at reducing perceived waiting time.

<HighlightList>
  <Highlight title="Speed" icon="speed">
    The elevator will travel up at 83 km/h (above which people get sick) and
    down at 36 km/h (above which people experience the sensation of falling).
    This reduces both actual and perceived waiting time.
  </Highlight>
  <Highlight title="Mirrors" icon="mirror">
    The shaft doors will feature mirrors. Occupied time feels shorter and people
    naturally tend to check their appearance. This classic trick reduces
    perceived waiting time.
  </Highlight>
  <Highlight title="Time" icon="time">
    Computer screens behind the semi‑transparent mirrors will display the
    current time. Seeing a clock counters the tendency to overestimate waiting
    time.
  </Highlight>
  <Highlight title="Weather" icon="sun">
    The opposite mirror door will show weather information, providing useful
    context and sparking conversation, which makes group waiting feel shorter.
  </Highlight>
  <Highlight title="Color" icon="paint">
    Cool colors (e.g., blue) will be used in the waiting area, as warm tones are
    linked to longer perceived wait times.
  </Highlight>
  <Highlight title="Light" icon="lightbulb">
    The waiting area will be dimly lit, as lower light levels are said to
    decrease perceived waiting time.
  </Highlight>
  <Highlight title="Scent" icon="air">
    A diffuser will emit a low‑concentration blend of fragrances linked to
    reducing anxiety, such as lavender and nutmeg.
  </Highlight>
  <Highlight title="Accessibility" icon="accessibility">
    Call buttons will be placed low enough for wheelchair users and children,
    provide tactile feedback, include braille, and be complemented by a voice UI
    announcing floor, elevator location, direction, and upcoming stops.
  </Highlight>
</HighlightList>

## Designing the visual interface

To complement the voice UI, I designed a visual interface that communicates the same information to sighted and hearing-impaired residents. The goal was to further reduce perceived waiting time by making system behavior transparent and predictable.

### Exploring directions

Through sketching, I explored several key design questions:

<HighlightList>
  <Highlight title="Orientation" icon="upDown">
    Should the UI be vertical (mirroring elevator movement) or horizontal
    (placed above the doors for better visibility)?
  </Highlight>
  <Highlight title="Floor indicator" icon="number">
    Numbers are precise but culturally ambiguous (ground-floor conventions,
    “unlucky” numbers). Shapes avoid this, but sacrifice detail.
  </Highlight>
  <Highlight title="Cause of stop" icon="stop">
    Showing whether a stop is for pickup or drop-off could shorten perceived
    waiting time, but risk visual clutter.
  </Highlight>
</HighlightList>

### Defining the core interface

High-fidelity explorations helped clarify which information was essential. I narrowed the interface to four elements:

<HighlightList>
  <Highlight icon="person">The floor the user is on</Highlight>
  <Highlight icon="number">The floor the elevator is on</Highlight>
  <Highlight icon="stop">The floors the elevator will stop on</Highlight>
  <Highlight icon="upDown">The direction of the elevator</Highlight>
</HighlightList>

Reducing the interface to these elements balanced clarity with simplicity, helping explain delays without overwhelming users.

### Validating and refining

To assess clarity, I tested two refined concepts with three participants. Both clearly communicated current floor and upcoming stops, but direction and the user’s own floor were not consistently understood.

<Image
  src={FirstTest}
  alt="Two high-fidelity elevator UI concepts. One has circle outlines around the floors the elevator will stop on and the floor the user is currently on is contained within a filled circle. The other concept has two rows of floor numbers: one for the floors the elevator will pass and the other for the floors the elevator will stop on. It features a circle outline around the floor the user is on."
  class="wrapper"

Based on this feedback, I refined the design and tested it with four new participants. Direction became obvious for all participants, though some still struggled to identify their own floor. A stronger floor indicator or additional signage near the elevator could resolve this remaining ambiguity.

## Outcome

Together, the environmental interventions (light, color, scent, mirrors), informational elements (time, weather), accessibility features (low tactile buttons and voice announcements), and the validated visual interface form a cohesive system designed to reduce perceived waiting time.

<Image
  src={Outcome}
  alt="A vector illustration of an elevator. There is a horizontal UI placed above the elevator doors. It shows the current floor, the floors the elevator will stop on, and the current floor the elevator is on and its direction. The elevator doors are reflective like mirrors and show the current weather and time. A large sign next to the elevator doors shows the current floor number. The elevator call buttons have braille text on them."
  class="wrapper"
/>

Overall, the proposal explores how layered, research-driven interventions might make the elevator experience feel smoother and more psychologically pleasant for residents of luxury apartments.

## Reflection

The biggest learning was how much reframing the problem shaped the outcome. Focusing on perceived waiting time rather than speed led me in directions I wouldn’t have explored otherwise.

If I were to continue the project, I would:

- Define measurable success criteria for perceived waiting time
- Prototype and test the full experience in a realistic waiting environment
- Narrow the intervention set to validate which elements have the greatest impact
