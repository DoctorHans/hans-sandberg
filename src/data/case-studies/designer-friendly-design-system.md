## TL;DR

I led an initiative at Siteimprove in 2021 to improve the UX of the design system for designers. Through surveys and interviews, I identified key UX gaps and turned them into a prioritized set of improvements across documentation, processes, and communication. The result: fewer repeat questions, smoother workflows, and a design system that felt more approachable and designer-friendly.

## Context

In 2021, I was a designer on Siteimprove's design systems team. The design system was intended to support both engineers and designers. However, observations and informal conversations suggested designers often felt underserved. I set out to better understand these issues and make the system feel more supportive of designers’ needs.

## Research

### Quantitative

My first step was to send out an anonymous survey to all designers to measure to what extent the design system was meeting its objectives.

The survey consisted of 17 questions across 5 categories. Most were phrased as statements. Designers rated each statement on a five‑point agreement scale.

**Example statements:**

- “It’s clear what the design system’s single source of truth is.”
- “I know what to do when a component I need is missing or doesn’t meet my needs.”
- “I feel welcome to contribute new or improved components.”

### Qualitative

To understand the “why” behind the survey data, I interviewed every designer one-on-one.

**For each issue, I asked:**

- **Have you experienced this?** — to validate pain points
- **How does it impact your work?** — to uncover workflow friction
- **How can we do better?** — to collect practical, context-aware suggestions

## Changes

### Make documentation more designer-oriented

A key finding was that the design system website was too engineering-focused. Designers found the content overly technical and had difficulty finding guidance relevant to their work.

In response, I restructured and expanded the documentation to better support designers:

- **Role-specific guidelines** — Split a single “Guidelines” section into dedicated sections for product designers, engineers, and content designers.
- **Icons FAQ** — Created a single source of truth for icon usage by introducing an up-to-date Icons FAQ.
- **Accessibility checklist** — Collaborated with accessibility experts to create a designer-focused accessibility checklist.

### Clarify the single source of truth

Another insight was that designers were sometimes unclear about what the source of truth was for components, guidelines, and design tokens.

I focused on making the source of truth clearer and easier to navigate:

- **Deprecated content warnings** — Added warnings to legacy pages that couldn’t be removed to prevent use of outdated guidance.
- **Reduced access friction** — Shortened the documentation URL to _fancy.siteimprove.com_, making it easier to find and remember.
- **Centralized guidelines** — Moved relevant, up-to-date content into the documentation site to reduce confusion and fragmentation.

### Standardize terminology and processes

Some design system terminology and processes were unclear and only explained informally.

- **Designer FAQ** — Introduced a centralized FAQ to document common terms and workflows in a single, accessible place.

### Keep designers informed

Designers sometimes felt out of the loop when it came to changes and additions to the design system.

I introduced two initiatives to improve visibility into ongoing changes:

- **Public changelogs** — Added designer-friendly changelogs to the documentation site, written for a non-technical audience.
- **More frequent updates** — Shared updates more regularly in a dedicated Slack channel for designers.

### Improve design review and feedback

Designers lacked clarity on how to get their designs reviewed by the design system team.

I collaborated with designers to define a new review-session format:

- **Multidisciplinary sessions** — Combined feedback from the design system team, accessibility experts, and UX writers.
- **Open participation** — Open to all designers to encourage shared learning and peer input.
- **Optional and informal** — Positioned as lightweight and non-mandatory to encourage participation.

## Impact

Although the project didn’t include a follow-up survey, multiple qualitative and behavioral signals pointed in a positive direction:

- Designers reported the documentation felt clearer and easier to navigate
- Slack questions about icons and guidelines decreased
- Contributors found guidance and processes more discoverable
- The design system team received fewer repeat questions
- Designers expressed appreciation and said the system now felt “more for us than before”

## Reflection

This project reinforced that design systems succeed or fail on communication, clarity, and consistency as much as on components or tokens. When designers know where to go, what to trust, and how to engage, the system becomes something they rely on rather than work around.
