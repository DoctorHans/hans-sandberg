## Context

In 2021, I was a designer on Siteimprove's design systems team. The design system was intended to support both engineers and designers. However, observations and informal conversations suggested designers often felt underserved. I set out to better understand this issue and make the system more supportive of designers’ needs.

## Research

### Quantitative

My first step was to send out an anonymous survey to all designers to measure to what extent the design system was meeting its objectives.

The survey consisted of 17 questions across 5 categories. Most were phrased as statements. Designers rated each statement on a five‑point agreement scale.

Here are some example statements:

<HighlightList>
  <Highlight blockquote icon="quote">
    It’s clear what the design system’s single source of truth is.
  </Highlight>
  <Highlight blockquote icon="quote">
    I know what to do when a component is missing or doesn’t meet my needs.
  </Highlight>
  <Highlight blockquote icon="quote">
    I feel welcome to contribute new or improved components.
  </Highlight>
</HighlightList>

### Qualitative

To understand the “why” behind the survey data, I interviewed every designer.

For each issue uncovered by the survey, I asked:

<HighlightList>
  <Highlight title="Have you experienced this?" icon="questionAnswer">
    To validate pain points.
  </Highlight>
  <Highlight title="How does it impact your work?" icon="work">
    To uncover workflow friction.
  </Highlight>
  <Highlight title="How can we do better?" icon="lightbulb">
    To collect practical, context-aware suggestions
  </Highlight>
</HighlightList>

## Changes

### Make documentation more designer-oriented

A key finding was that the design system website was too engineering-focused. Designers found the content overly technical and had difficulty finding guidance relevant to their work.

In response, I restructured and expanded the documentation to better support designers:

<HighlightList>
  <Highlight title="Role-specific guidelines" icon="groups">
    Split a single “Guidelines” section into dedicated sections for product
    designers, engineers, and content designers.
  </Highlight>
  <Highlight title="Icons FAQ" icon="help">
    Created a single source of truth for icon usage by introducing an up-to-date
    Icons FAQ.
  </Highlight>
  <Highlight title="Accessibility checklist" icon="checklist">
    Collaborated with accessibility experts to create a designer-focused
    accessibility checklist.
  </Highlight>
</HighlightList>

### Clarify the single source of truth

Another insight was that designers were sometimes unclear about what the source of truth was for components, guidelines, and design tokens.

I focused on making the source of truth clearer and easier to navigate:

<HighlightList>
  <Highlight title="Deprecated content warnings" icon="warning">
    Added warnings to legacy pages that couldn’t be removed to prevent use of
    outdated guidance
  </Highlight>
  <Highlight title="Reduced access friction" icon="link">
    Shortened the documentation URL to _fancy.siteimprove.com_, making it easier
    to find and remember.
  </Highlight>
  <Highlight title="Centralized guidelines" icon="hub">
    Moved relevant, up-to-date content into the documentation site to reduce
    confusion and fragmentation.
  </Highlight>
</HighlightList>

Some design system terminology and processes were unclear and only explained informally.

<Highlight title="Designer FAQ" listItem={false} icon="help">

  single, accessible place.

### Keep designers informed

Designers sometimes felt out of the loop when it came to changes and additions to the design system.

I introduced two initiatives to improve visibility into ongoing changes:

<HighlightList>
  <Highlight title="Public changelogs" icon="history">
    Added designer-friendly changelogs to the documentation site, written for a
    non-technical audience.
  </Highlight>
  <Highlight title="More frequent updates" icon="announcement">
    Shared updates more regularly in a dedicated Slack channel for designers.
  </Highlight>
</HighlightList>

### Improve design review and feedback

Designers lacked clarity on how to get their designs reviewed by the design system team.

I collaborated with designers to define a new review-session format:

<HighlightList>
  <Highlight title="Multidisciplinary sessions" icon="groups">
    Combined feedback from the design system team, accessibility experts, and UX
    writers.
  </Highlight>
  <Highlight title="Open participation" icon="meetingRoom">
    Open to all designers to encourage shared learning and peer input.
  </Highlight>
  <Highlight title="Optional and informal" icon="coffee">
    Positioned as lightweight and non-mandatory to encourage participation.
  </Highlight>
</HighlightList>

## Impact

Although the project didn’t include a follow-up survey, multiple qualitative and behavioral signals pointed in a positive direction:

<HighlightList>
  <Highlight title="Clearer documentation" icon="menuBook">
    Designers reported the documentation felt clearer and easier to navigate.
  </Highlight>
  <Highlight title="Fewer questions" icon="questionAnswer">
    Questions about icons, guidelines, and design system processes decreased,
    reducing support overhead for the design system team.
  </Highlight>
  <Highlight title="More designer ownership" icon="heart">
    Designers expressed appreciation and said the system now felt{" "}
    <q>more for us than before</q>.
  </Highlight>
</HighlightList>

## Reflection

This project reinforced that design systems succeed or fail on communication, clarity, and consistency as much as on components or tokens. When designers know where to go, what to trust, and how to engage, the system becomes something they rely on rather than work around.
